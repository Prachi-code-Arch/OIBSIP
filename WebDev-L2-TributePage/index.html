A Tribute To

Ada Lovelace

The Enchantress of Numbers — who saw a calculating machine and imagined a computer.

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace · 1815–1852

A Life in Numbers

Born in London in 1815, Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, was the only legitimate child of the poet Lord Byron. Her mother, Lady Byron, fearing her daughter might inherit her father's volatile temperament, arranged for Ada to be tutored rigorously in mathematics and logic from an early age — a highly unusual education for a woman of her time.

In 1833, at just seventeen, Ada met the mathematician and inventor Charles Babbage and was captivated by his designs for the Analytical Engine, a mechanical general-purpose computer that would not be built in his lifetime. Where others saw an elaborate calculator, Ada saw something further reaching: a machine capable of manipulating symbols according to rules, not merely crunching numbers.

Between 1842 and 1843, Ada translated an Italian paper on the Analytical Engine and appended her own extensive notes — nearly three times the length of the original text. Within them, she described an algorithm for the machine to compute Bernoulli numbers, now widely regarded as the first published computer program, decades before any such machine existed.

Ada Lovelace died in 1852 at the age of thirty-six, never seeing her ideas realized in metal and gears. It would take more than a century, and the arrival of the electronic computer, for the significance of her insight to be fully understood. Today she is remembered as one of computing's first visionaries.

Punched Into History

Key moments, recorded ledger-style — a nod to the punched cards that once instructed mechanical engines.

  1. 1815

    Born in London

    Daughter of poet Lord Byron and mathematician Annabella Milbanke.

  2. 1833

    Meets Charles Babbage

    Introduced to Babbage's Difference Engine at a London gathering, sparking a lifelong fascination.

  3. 1843

    Publishes Her Notes

    Her annotations on the Analytical Engine include what is now considered the first published algorithm intended for a machine.

  4. 1852

    Passes Away

    Dies at thirty-six, her contributions largely unrecognized in her own lifetime.

  5. 1980

    Language Named in Her Honor

    The U.S. Department of Defense names its new programming language "Ada" in her memory.
